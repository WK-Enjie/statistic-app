Types of Data

📊 Quantitative (Numerical)

  • Discrete: Countable values (e.g., number of students)
  • Continuous: Measurable values (e.g., height, weight)

📝 Qualitative (Categorical)

  • Nominal: Categories with no order (e.g., blood type)
  • Ordinal: Categories with order (e.g., satisfaction rating)

⚖️ Advantages & Disadvantages of Statistical Representations

Understanding the strengths and weaknesses of each type helps you choose the right one and answer exam questions.

📊 Summary Comparison Table

Representation Best For Key Advantage Key Disadvantage
Bar Graph Categorical / Discrete data Easy to compare categories Not for continuous data
Pictogram Simple categorical data Visually engaging Hard to show exact values
Line Graph Trends over time Shows trends clearly May imply continuity
Pie Chart Proportions of a whole Shows proportions clearly Hard to compare small slices
Dot Diagram Small discrete data sets Shows every data point Cluttered for large sets
Histogram Continuous grouped data Shows distribution shape Individual values lost
Stem-and-Leaf Small to medium numerical data Retains all original values Not for large data sets
Cumulative Frequency Finding median, quartiles, percentiles Easy to read median & quartiles Doesn't show individual frequencies
Box-and-Whisker Comparing data sets, spread Shows 5-number summary at a glance Doesn't show distribution shape details

⚠️ Misinterpretation of Statistical Diagrams

Statistical diagrams can be misleading (intentionally or accidentally).

🔍 Drawing Inferences from Statistical Diagrams

Drawing inferences means making conclusions or observations based on the data shown.

What to Look For

1 Trends: Is the data increasing, decreasing, or staying constant?
2 Clusters: Where is most of the data concentrated?
3 Outliers: Are there any unusual values far from the rest?
4 Comparisons: How do different groups compare?
5 Spread: Is the data tightly clustered or widely spread?
6 Symmetry / Skewness: Is the distribution symmetric or skewed?

Example Inference Statements

  • "Most students scored between 60 and 79 marks."
  • "There is a general increasing trend in sales from January to June."
  • "The data is positively skewed, indicating most values are on the lower end."
  • "Student A was more consistent as shown by the smaller IQR."
  • "The obvious outlier at 15 suggests this could be an error or unusual result."
